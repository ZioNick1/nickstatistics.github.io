Gambler's Ruin Problem

Description of Gambler's Ruin Problem

The Gambler's Ruin problem is a famous concept in probability theory. A persistent gambler who constantly raises his bet to a fixed fraction every time he loses will eventually go broke in a finite amount of bets. This problem is often modelled with a random walk . In this model, the gambler will return to the point of origin of the walk. In simple terms will go broke.

Reasons for the results

In a fair game let \(d\) be the amount of money that a gambler has at disposal at the beginning of the game. If he raises the bet of \(\frac{d}{N}\) every time he wins but does not reduce it when he loses, it will take at most N bets to end bankrupt, however big N is. By playing a fair game (let's say a toss coin), the gambler has chance of winning of \(\frac{1}{2}\) on the first bet. And by simmetry the same chance of going broke. If he repeats the process, he has the same \(\frac{1}{2}\) chance of going broke. Hence at the second process he has chance of \((\frac{1}{2})^2 = \frac{1}{4}\) chance of not going broke. This process repeats for N times. Then we will have

\(\sum_{i = 1}^{N} (\frac{1}{2})^i\)
chance that he goes broke which approaches 1.
