Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

The Central Limit Theorem is a fundamental concept in probability theory and statistics. It states that the distribution of the sum (or average) of a large number of independent, identically distributed random variables approaches a normal distribution, regardless of the underlying population distribution.

Statement of CLT

Let X₁, X₂, X₃, ... be a sequence of independent and identically distributed random variables with mean (μ) and variance (σ²). Then, as n (the sample size) becomes large:

X̄n ≈ N(μ, σ²/n)

Key Points:

  • The CLT is valid for both discrete and continuous random variables.
  • The larger the sample size (n), the closer the sample mean X̄n is to a normal distribution.
  • The CLT is a powerful tool for statistical inference, as it allows us to make inferences about the population even when we don't know the population's distribution.

Proof of Central Limit Theorem (Simplified)

The full proof of the Central Limit Theorem involves complex mathematical concepts, such as characteristic functions. Here, we provide a simplified outline:

  1. Start with the moment generating function (MGF) of X̄n:

    2. M(t) = E[e^(tX̄n)]

  2. Use properties of expectations:

    3. M(t) = E[e^(t/n * (X₁ + X₂ + ... + Xn))]

  3. Assume MGF exists for X₁, X₂, ..., and they are i.i.d.:

    4. M(t) = [M(t/n)]ⁿ

  4. Take the limit as n → ∞:

    5. lim(n → ∞) M(t) = e^(σ²t²/2)

  5. Recognize the MGF of a normal distribution:

    6. M(t) = e^(μt + σ²t²/2)

  6. Conclude that X̄n ≈ N(μ, σ²/n) based on the MGF of a normal distribution.

The Central Limit Theorem is a cornerstone of statistics, providing a bridge between the behavior of large samples and the properties of the normal distribution.
